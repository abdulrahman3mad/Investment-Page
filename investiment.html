Business is a passion, but it requires more than that to grow.

So where does venture capital and private equity fit in?

So what is venture capital?

In the UK particularly, Venture capital provides finance and operational expertise for entrepreneurs and exciting start-up companies.VC is about early-stage investing, and it’s about innovative business propositions with strong growth potential and disruption in its DNA.

This means VCs take on substantial risks and can be faced with the decision of continuing to fund an investee company if it’s failing, or to make the hard choice to let it fail. It’s an investment strategy based on high performance and high returns from rapidly growing businesses.

And what about private equity

As an investment model private equity is a driver of sustainable business growth.It typically supports buyouts and management buy-ins in mature companies, where the PE takes a majority stake. PEs have an investment horizon of ten up to ten years for each portfolio or fund, allowing them to develop efficiencies with in the portfolio and to extract value through growth and through streamlined cost management, and ultimately via the sale of each investee company at an increased value.

PEs are of ten sector agnostic and will focus more on the operational and market fundamentals of a potential buyout or investment, leading to mixed and sometimes hedged portfolios within and across their funds.Once the capital from each fund is deployed across a number of investments, the PE starts over by raising for their next fund and commencing the portfolio building process once again.

PrivateEquity vs.VentureCapital

Opposite Investment Mindsets?

Managers of private equity (PE) and venture capital (VC) firms typically have the same goal in mind - to maximise returns through investment. Yet PE are typically buyout funds and VC are typically early-stage funds, and they go about choosing investments in very different ways.

Many prospective investors fail to appreciate that these two popular alternative asset classes adopt opposing, antithetical methods to drive performance. This diametric face-off in portfolio structure pivots around one particular element of fund management-their diversification strategy.

PE and VC firms alike follow atwo-pronged investment strategy to optimise both portfolio diversification and holding periods:

Private Equity

The PE fund managers with the highest returns are free booting financial engineers who bet big, bag easy proceed sex peditiously, and realize investment failures slowly. They carry out quick flips and dividend recaps with their most stellar investees while holding onto lack luster assets for as long as creditors allow. In this way, they seek to turn these as sets around and also cushion the negative impact they may have onthe fund’s blended returns. Hence the many buyout zombies wallowing in a state of aimless lethargy.

Private Equity

Venture Capital

The best-performing VCs have along pedigree as business builders that make numerous small bets by backing start-ups with potential that sometimes fail, and who shed their worst portfolio assets swiftly. Hence the incentive to aggressively boost valuations and hunt for exponential growth to compensate for the many losers.

As aptly quoted by the CFA Institute venture capitalists are ‘swashbucklers that seek business risk’, particularly disruption, and they champion in novation to generate long-term economic value. Buyout specialists pile up financial risk, or leverage, and perform liquidity tricks to play the TVM game.

Careers in Venture Capital and Private Equit

PEs and VCs try to find their deal-makers from similar pools of candidates, such as investment bankers. Bank work involves firm valuation, which is an important procedure that helps PEs in particular reassure themselves that they are making a good investment.

PEs and VCs hire experts in particular industries consulting or working full-time with them.They may also recruit from top management consulting firms to improve the quality of their investments.

Private Equity

  • 1. PEs are more likely to attract investment bankers
  • 2. At interviews, PE recruiters focus more on the number of deals you have been involved with and how good you are with financial modeling
  • 3. In work and culture, PEs resemble investment banks—the hours are long, there’s a lot of coordination on deals, and much technical analysis on Excel
  • 4. The skills that PE recruiters look for are analytical ability, background in banking, consulting, or corporate development; talent for research; knowledge of financial modeling; and knowledge of various industries.
  • 5. In PE firms particularly, analysts aim at getting to partner level so they can earn carry.To go higher up the ladder, analysts need the ability to source good deals, have strong relation ships with investment banks to get financing, and the ability to recruit good management for portfolio companies. In the junior phase of your career, you may only need to be good atanalysis, but if you want to progress your career then networking assumes increasingly more importance as you focus on sourcing and closing deals for the portfolio
  • 6. PE work is not much different from investment bank work. The focus is on deal transactions. The tasks include sourcing deals, forecasting returns, meeting bankers and private lenders for debt financing, reviewing legal documents, closing deals, coordinating funds flow, assisting the CEO/CFO with financial reports, and preparing the company for sale when financial targets have been achieved.

Venture Capital

  • 1. VCs draw a variety of professionals such as business development experts, product managers, consultants, former entrepreneurs, and even bankers.
  • 2. VCs tend to examine your networking qualities, your understanding of markets, and your ability to bring in deals.
  • 3. VCs involve more networking and meetings, but the hours are kinder and the workplace is more relaxed.
  • 4. As for VCs, people from varied backgrounds get alook-in. You require good deal-experience, good communication skills, and the ability to source investment opportunities. It helps if you have sound knowledge of technology/biotech or health care fields and can convince the company that you are interested in various industries, including start-ups.
